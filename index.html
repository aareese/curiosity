Curiosity on Mars

On August 5th, 2012, the Mars Science Laboratory Curiosity rover dropped out of the sky and onto the dusty red floor of Gale Crater. For the next several weeks, the 2000-pound machine stretched its legs, spun its wheels, and revved its nuclear engine, all in preparation for the most ambitious remotely-operated scientific expedition ever attempted.

The Final Frontier

The Curiosity Rover is the forefront of humanity’s quest to understand the universe. This stuff is in our genes, the need to push the boundaries and climb peaks just to see what’s on the other side. Over the millennia, this exploratory instinct led our species to first fan out across the globe, and then re-connect, as explorers sailed across expansive seas just to find their distant relatives waiting on the shores. Only recently has technology allowed us to step off our terrestrial lifeboat, sending people and robotic emissaries to the Moon and on to other planets. But still, our sense of wonder persists as we dive deeper into the scientific assessment of our planetary neighbor: What was Mars like in eons past? Could it have supported life during its warmer and wetter past? Could it still?


And as the Mars Science Laboratory team scours the red dust for answers, one question looms above all: Where will our Curiosity take us next?
